Frequently Asked Questions

  • 1. What is a genetic counselor?

    Genetic counselors are health professionals with specialized graduate degrees and experience in the areas of medical genetics and counseling. Genetic counselors work as members of a health care team, providing information and support to families who have members with genetic disorders and to families who may be at risk for a variety of inherited conditions. They identify families at risk, investigate the problem present in the family, interpret information about the disorder, analyze inheritance patterns and risks of recurrence and review available options with the family.

    Genetic counselors also provide supportive counseling to families, serve as patient advocates and refer individuals and families to community or state support services. They serve as educators and resource people for other health care professionals and for the general public.

  • 2. Why licensure for genetic counselors?

    Licensure ensures the protection of Tennessee health care consumers seeking genetic counseling services by ensuring that appropriately qualified professionals provide genetic counseling and mandating continuing education for providers of genetic counseling services. Licensure also promotes increased awareness by both consumers and health care providers of the genetic counseling services available in Tennessee.

  • 3. Does licensure for genetic counselors affect nurses and physicians who perform genetic counseling?

    The licensure of Genetic Counselors in Tennessee and other states is not designed to prevent licensed health care providers from providing patient services within their scope of practice. Physicians, nurses and other health care professionals providing genetic services under their respective licenses can continue doing so.

  • 4. What role do genetic counselors play in the State of Tennessee?

    The state of Tennessee has 37 genetic counselors. Genetic counselors in Tennessee work in a variety of settings, including university-based medical centers and private hospitals. Genetic counselors work with many different health care teams, including cancer, pediatrics, obstetrics, and research.

  • 5. Do other states have licensure for genetic counselors?

    Currently 18 states have legislation for genetic counselor licensure: California, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Indiana, Massachusetts, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New Mexico, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Pennslyvania, South Dakota, Tennessee, Utah, and Washington. About three other states are actively pursing licensure in their state.

  • 6. Don't genetic counselors already have certification?

    Genetic counselors can receive certification from the American Board of Genetic Counselors. Certification is not an enforceable standard. Even though certification requires appropriate education and training, it is not mandatory nor is it legally enforceable. Licensure ensures the responsible practice of genetic counselors in Tennessee, protecting the consumer from harm.

  • 7. Where can I get more information about genetic counseling?

    More information about genetic counseling can be found at the website of the National Society of Genetic Counselors.
